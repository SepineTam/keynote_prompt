Speaker Notes:
Ladies and gentlemen, esteemed colleagues, today we delve into a groundbreaking study that challenges our understanding of gender imbalance in China. We'll explore how the price of tea, a seemingly unrelated economic factor, significantly impacts the phenomenon of "missing women." This presentation is based on the seminal work of Professor Nancy Qian, published in the Quarterly Journal of Economics in 2008.

Speaker Notes:
Let's begin by establishing the research context. The "Missing Women" phenomenon, first identified by Amartya Sen, refers to the deficit of women in populations where we would expect gender parity. In China, this manifests as a severe gender imbalance, with significantly more males than females. This slide shows the sex ratio at birth in China as of 2020: 118.06 males per 100 females, far above the natural ratio of about 105. This imbalance sits at the intersection of economics and demography, challenging us to consider how economic factors might influence demographic outcomes in unexpected ways.

Speaker Notes:
The central question of this research is: How do sex-specific earnings impact gender ratios? Professor Qian ingeniously uses the tea industry as a lens to examine this question. Why tea? Firstly, women have a comparative advantage in tea picking due to the delicate nature of the work. Secondly, fluctuations in tea prices provide a natural experiment, allowing us to observe how changes in female-specific earning potential affect gender outcomes. It's worth noting the scale of China's tea industry: in 2020, China produced 2.97 million metric tons of tea, highlighting the potential widespread impact of this economic factor on demographics.

Speaker Notes:
The theoretical framework of this study is rooted in the household decision model. It views gender selection through an economic lens, considering children as "investment goods." The simplified utility function shown here illustrates how households might make decisions based on consumption, the number of male and female children, and child quality. This model helps us understand how changes in the economic value of female children, due to increased earning potential in the tea industry, might alter household decisions and ultimately affect gender ratios.

Speaker Notes:
Now, let's delve into the data and methodology. This study focuses on tea-growing regions in China, spanning from the 1970s to the 1990s. The key variables include sex ratios, tea prices, and the suitability of areas for tea planting. Professor Qian employs a Difference-in-Differences (DID) approach, which allows us to compare changes in gender ratios between tea-producing and non-tea-producing regions before and after tea price changes. The model specification shown here illustrates how this method isolates the effect of tea prices on gender outcomes, controlling for various factors and fixed effects.
